01.
Language Profile

An endangered, heavily Hispanicised Malayo-Polynesian language whose precise position within Austronesian remains unresolved.

TL;DR Chamorro is spoken in Guam and the Northern Mariana Islands, with around 58 000 speakers across an island and diaspora population. Four centuries of colonial contact have heavily Hispanicised its lexicon and parts of its functional morphology, but the Austronesian morphosyntactic core remains intact.

Chamorro is an Austronesian language spoken natively on the island of Guam and in the Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands (CNMI), with diaspora communities in Hawai'i and the continental United States, concentrated in California and Washington state. Its ISO 639-3 code is cha and its Glottocode is cham1312 (Hammarström et al. 2024). The language is written in the Latin script, with two slightly varying orthographies in use in Guam and the CNMI (Chung 2020: 671–675). We follow the spelling conventions of Chung (2020) and Topping (1973) throughout.

1.1Classification

The internal classification of Chamorro within Austronesian is contested. It is consistently treated as Malayo-Polynesian, but its position within that branch is unresolved. Costenoble (1940) argued for Philippine affinities on lexical and morphological grounds, and a similar view is defended in different forms by Starosta & Pagotto (1991) and Zobel (2002). Reid (2002: 91–92) reanalyses much of this evidence as the result of contact rather than shared inheritance, treating Chamorro as a first-order branch of Proto Extra-Formosan that separated very early from the rest of the family. Blust (2000) likewise treats Chamorro as standing outside the main Oceanic lineage. For the purposes of this portfolio we follow Chung (2020: 3), who treats Chamorro's subgrouping within Western Malayo-Polynesian as unresolved and not closely related to the Micronesian or Polynesian languages of its geographical neighbours.

1.2Speakers and dialects

Ethnologue (2024) estimates approximately 58 000 speakers in total: 29 000 in Guam (against an ethnic Chamorro population of around 62 700), 12 000 in the CNMI, and a further 17 000 in the US-mainland diaspora. Two dialects are recognised — Chamorro proper (across Guam and most of the CNMI) and Rotanese Chamorro (spoken on the island of Rota) (Ethnologue 2024; Chung 2020: 5–7).

1.3Contact history

Chamorro has been a contact language for much of its documented history. Solenberger (1962) describes the mid-twentieth-century Marianas as a layered multilingual ecology in which Chamorro coexisted with Spanish, German, Japanese and English, each associated with successive colonial periods: Spanish administration from the late seventeenth century until 1898; American annexation of Guam in 1898; German rule in the CNMI from 1899 to 1914; and Japanese rule in the CNMI from 1914 to 1944. Stolz & Stolz (2001) describe Chamorro as one of the most heavily Hispanicised languages of the Pacific, with extensive lexical borrowing and the incorporation of some functional morphology — including the comparative particle mås and the standard marker kinu discussed in §4. The Austronesian morphosyntactic base of the language has nonetheless remained largely intact.

1.4Endangerment

Chamorro is endangered, with the severity of the situation varying by territory. Ethnologue (2024) classifies the language as EGIDS level 2 (Provincial) in Guam, where it has had statutory recognition since 1974; level 3 (Wider Communication) in the CNMI, where it is co-official with English and Carolinian under the 1978 constitution; and level 6b (Threatened) in the US-mainland diaspora. Glottolog (Campbell et al. 2022) assigns the language an agglomerated endangerment status of 'shifting'. The principal driver of decline is disrupted intergenerational transmission. Chung (2020: 2) describes an abrupt shift from Chamorro to English among children in Saipan in the 1980s, and observes that by 2010 the situation in the CNMI had come to resemble Guam's a decade earlier, with fluent speakers concentrated among older generations. Solenberger's (1962: 63) mid-century prediction that Chamorro would survive as the language of mother–child transmission across the Marianas has therefore been only partially borne out.

1.5Documentary record

The documentary record for Chamorro is comparatively rich. The principal reference grammar is Chung's (2020) 728-page open-access Chamorro Grammar, which serves as the primary source for this portfolio and supersedes the earlier reference grammar by Topping (1973). Topping (1973) remains useful for its discussion of older forms and is accompanied by a Chamorro–English dictionary (Topping, Ogo & Dungca 1975). Safford's (1903–1905) five-part article The Chamorro Language of Guam is the earliest substantial descriptive source. Cooreman's (1987) monograph on transitivity and ergativity in Chamorro narratives is the standard reference for the alignment-related material discussed in §3. Crosslinguistic data are drawn from WALS (Dryer & Haspelmath 2013) under language code cha. Chamorro has been taught as a compulsory subject in primary schools in Guam since Guam Public Law 31–45 in 2011, and as a school subject at primary and secondary level in the CNMI, with active language-development programmes in both jurisdictions.

Word Order

A flexible VSO language that becomes rigid under ambiguity and is consistently head-initial across every phrasal category.

TL;DR Chamorro prefers VSO but tolerates VOS when context disambiguates. The verb, the noun, and the preposition all precede their dependents — a textbook head-initial profile, conforming to Greenberg's Universal #3.

Research on Chamorro word order has been dominated by the work of Sandra Chung, whose studies provide detailed analyses of the language's syntax and syntax–semantics interface (Chung 1998, 2020). Chamorro is classified as a verb-initial language with a 'flexible VSO' structure (Chung 1998: 23): while it strongly prefers VSO, certain sentence structures heavily imply that VOS is permissible in some instances.

Cross-linguistically, VSO is the third most common word order, occurring in 9.2% of languages surveyed by Song (2018: 239, Table 10.3). In the same study — a sample of 402 languages drawn from 1 063 — 44.78% were SOV and 41.79% SVO, illustrating the comparative rarity of verb-initial orders.

No quantitative data on the frequency of different word orders within Chamorro itself appears to exist, so the language is categorised as VSO on descriptive grounds. This VSO word order is most commonly seen in transitive clauses (Chung 1998: 21), as in (1).

2.2Head-directionality

The Head-Directionality Parameter states that the head of a phrase must either precede or follow its complement. Chung (2020: 169) classifies Chamorro as both head-initial and predicate-initial: heads and predicates consistently precede their arguments.

2.5Discussion

Linking back to Greenberg's (1963) typological universals, Chamorro conforms to Universal #3: 'Languages with dominant VSO order are always prepositional' (Greenberg 1963: 78). The prepositional examples seen above corroborate this, and no postpositional construction is attested in Chamorro. The language therefore fits the predicted profile cleanly.

03.
Alignment

A mood-conditioned split: ergative-absolutive in realis, nominative-accusative in irrealis. The nominal system, by contrast, is neutral.

TL;DR Full noun phrases carry no overt case (neutral alignment). In realis verbal agreement, only A is cross-referenced for person — S patterns with P. In irrealis, both S and A are cross-referenced together — accusative-style. A productive antipassive (man-/fan-) clinches the ergative analysis of the realis side.

Chamorro shows a split-ergative alignment system conditioned by the realis/irrealis mood distinction (Cooreman 1987: 36; Zobel 2002: 410; Chung 2020: 175–200). The analysis below treats three loci of alignment marking in turn — nominal case, verbal person marking, and the pronominal system — and follows Cooreman and Chung in concluding that nominal case is neutral, verbal person marking is split-ergative, and the weak-pronoun system reflects the verbal split. The section closes with the antipassive, which is typologically diagnostic of ergative systems.

3.1Nominal case marking

Chamorro has a morphological distinction between an unmarked case, an oblique case, and a local case (Chung 2020: 87). However, 'the unmarked case is used for noun phrases that are subjects or direct objects' (Chung 2020: 91). The A, S and P arguments of a verb all appear in the unmarked case when they are full NPs. Nominal case therefore instantiates neutral alignment, and on its own it cannot distinguish A from P.

3.2Verbal person marking — realis

Chamorro distinguishes realis from irrealis mood morphologically. In realis clauses, the verb agrees with A in both person and number through a proclitic paradigm (e.g. ha- '3SG'). By contrast, S is marked only for number, via the alternation between the realis intransitive infix ‹um› (singular) and the prefix man- (plural). No person information is encoded on S. In irrealis clauses, S and A are marked in the same way, to the exclusion of P. This is nominative-accusative alignment. Because the verbal person system is ergative-absolutive in realis but nominative-accusative in irrealis, Chamorro has a genuinely split alignment system, conditioned by mood.

3.4The pronominal system

Chamorro has two sets of personal pronouns, traditionally called 'strong' and 'weak' (Chung 1998: 28; 2020: 175–180). Strong pronouns are semantically and pragmatically marked, occurring in focus, emphasis, and as prepositional complements. Weak pronouns are the unmarked option. Weak pronouns such as yo' 'I/me' can only appear where they are not cross-referenced by verbal agreement — that is, as S in realis clauses, and as P in both realis and irrealis clauses (Zobel 2002: 410). In irrealis intransitive clauses, where verbal agreement encodes a first-person plural subject, an overt weak pronoun cannot appear, as shown in (15).

3.5The antipassive

Chamorro has a productive antipassive construction (Cooreman 1988; Chung 2020: ch. 10), formed with the prefix man- (realis) or fan- (irrealis). The antipassive is typologically diagnostic of ergative alignment: it detransitivises a clause by promoting A to S and demoting P to an optional oblique. In (16a) the transitive Un-patek 'you kicked' takes i ga'lagu 'the dog' as its P; in the antipassive (16b), hao is now S and the patient appears in an oblique phrase.

3.6Crosslinguistic context

Chamorro's nominal alignment is crosslinguistically common: 52% of the 190 languages in Comrie's (2013a, WALS ch. 98) sample show neutral case-marking on full NPs. For pronoun alignment, Comrie (2013b, WALS feature 99A) codes Chamorro as ergative-absolutive — making it the only language in his sample that combines neutral alignment on full NPs with ergative alignment on pronouns. This unusual coding reflects the system described in §3.4, where weak pronouns are distributed by the ergative verbal-agreement pattern. The verbal alignment is rarer still: Siewierska (2013, WALS ch. 100) codes only around 5–7% of her sample as ergative or split, and Chamorro's specifically mood-conditioned, ergative/accusative split is only one sub-pattern. Split-ergativity conditioned by the imperfective/realis vs. perfective/irrealis distinction is associated especially with some Indo-Aryan and Kartvelian languages (e.g. Hindi-Urdu, Georgian). Chamorro's split is unusual among Austronesian languages in being so clearly morphological rather than semantic, and remains a productive site for typological research.

DeLancey (1981) proposes that split-ergativity reflects a natural attention-flow asymmetry tied to aspect. In perfective clauses, the speaker's attention rests on the patient, so A is singled out for special marking; in imperfective clauses, attention rests on the agent, so P is singled out instead. Whether Chamorro's realis/irrealis distinction maps straightforwardly onto DeLancey's perfective/imperfective split is not obvious, but a rough comparison is informative. In realis, A alone receives person agreement (ergative patterning), consistent with DeLancey's prediction that the perfective-like mood should single out A. The accusative half in irrealis, however, does not follow straightforwardly from DeLancey's model — which predicts that the imperfective-like mood should single out P rather than leaving it unmarked relative to a shared S/A pattern. The Chamorro data are therefore at best partially compatible with DeLancey's account, and do not on their own confirm or refute it.

04.
Comparison

Comparative ordering is overtly encoded — a textbook 'explicit' strategy in Stassen's typology — but three of the four exponents have been borrowed from Spanish.

TL;DR Chamorro marks comparison with a native suffix -ña, a Spanish-derived particle mås 'more', and a Spanish-derived standard marker kinu/ki 'than'. Equatives use achá-, superlatives mås … i / itmås. Differential measure phrases (mås menus 'much less') are allowed, confirming the explicit-gradable analysis.

Chamorro expresses comparative orderings using an explicit strategy (Stassen 2013). A greater-than relation between a comparee and a standard is overtly encoded by dedicated functional morphology: a comparative suffix -ña on the dimension, or one of the free particles mås 'more' and menus 'less', together with a standard marker kinu ~ ki 'than' (Topping 1973: 207; Chung 2020: 570–578). The construction is overtly morphological and overtly relational, distinguishing Chamorro from languages with a purely implicit (conjoined) comparative strategy.

The suffix -ña is syncretic with a possessor-agreement suffix (see §5); in the comparative it is analysable as a degree morpheme rather than a possessive. A morphological argument for treating it as genuinely comparative — not just a degree intensifier — is that -ña is incompatible with equative and superlative readings: equatives use the prefix achá-, and superlatives use mås or itmås.

4.2Attributive and adverbial comparatives

The particle mås is more flexible than the suffix. It can modify an adjective in an attributive construction (as in mås meggai apatcha' 'more brown-spotted grasshoppers'), an intransitive verb in an adverbial construction (sigi ha' mås ma'åomenta i tiningo'-hu 'my knowledge keeps expanding'), or a locative adverbial (påpa' mås ni gaigi i lanchu 'farther down is where the farm is') — see Chung (2020: 573–576) for further examples.

4.4Contact and origins

Three of the four key exponents — mås 'more', kinu/ki 'than' and itmås 'most' — are Spanish borrowings from más, que, and el más respectively (Stolz & Stolz 2001; Rodríguez-Ponga 1995). Only the suffix -ña and the equative prefix achá- are unambiguously inherited. This makes Chamorro a useful case study in how contact can replace or duplicate native comparative morphology without, apparently, changing the typology of the construction (which remains explicit, standard-marked, and graded).

4.5Evaluation

The analysis distinguishes two semantic strategies for comparison (cf. Stassen 2013, WALS ch. 121). Under the explicit strategy, dedicated functional morphology directly encodes the ordering relation between comparee and standard. Under the implicit strategy, the ordering is not encoded but inferred — a sentence says that a property holds of the comparee and does not hold of the standard, without ever asserting that one exceeds the other. The mapping between form and strategy is not one-to-one: Nez Perce has a morphological comparative particle qetu 'more', but Deal & Hohaus (2019) argue that the language nonetheless uses an implicit strategy, since it lacks differential comparatives, measure constructions, and degree questions. The presence of comparative morphology is therefore not on its own sufficient evidence for the explicit strategy.

This makes the Chamorro evidence for an explicit analysis particularly telling. Chamorro not only has dedicated comparative morphology (-ña, mås, kinu/ki) but also permits differential measure phrases such as mås menus 'much less' and superlative constructions with itmås. These are the constructions that are unavailable in implicit-strategy languages like Nez Perce. Their availability in Chamorro supports an analysis in which the comparative encodes a genuine scalar ordering, not a coarser partition into 'X is A, Y is not A'. Chamorro therefore falls clearly on the explicit side of Stassen's typology. A remaining question is whether the suffix -ña itself contributes the comparative meaning compositionally or whether the ordering is introduced by a silent degree operator — answering it would require the kind of acceptability judgements that are not available from the published descriptive sources.

05.
Possession

Head-marking throughout: a possessor-agreement suffix on the head noun, and a general linker -n when the possessor is a full NP. Alienability is a gradient, not a binary.

TL;DR Chamorro is head-marking in the nominal domain (Nichols 1986). Pronominal possessors are realised as suffixes on the possessed head; full-NP possessors are introduced by the general linker -n. Body-part and kinship terms take only the suffix (inalienable end of Nichols's hierarchy); historically a separate dependent-noun system (iyo- / gâ- / na'-) marked the alienable end.

Adnominal possession in Chamorro is organised around two main structural resources: a possessor-agreement suffix on the head noun (used when the possessor is pronominal or the relation is inalienable), and a linker -n (sometimes =n) that relates the head noun to a following full-NP possessor. Following Nichols (1986) and Nichols & Bickel (2013, WALS ch. 24–25), this makes Chamorro a head-marking language in the nominal domain: the morphology encoding the possessive relation sits on the possessed head, not on the dependent possessor.

Chung (2020: 142) notes that the linker is not restricted to possessive contexts. It is 'a morpheme used more generally in Chamorro to indicate that a head has a modifier or other dependent', appearing in compound formations, noun–adjective sequences with a non-agreeing modifier, and other head-dependent configurations. The possessive interpretation is therefore compositional — supplied by the lexical semantics of the two nouns rather than by the linker itself. Chamorro does not formally grammaticalise a genitive case distinct from other linker-marked relations.

The Chamorro alienability split is more subtle than a simple zero-coded vs. overtly-coded contrast. It is tempting to say that inalienable possession is less marked (the possessee takes only a pronominal suffix) while alienable possession is more marked (with a linker), consistent with Haspelmath's (2008, 2017) generalisation that if one of the two constructions carries more overt marking, it will be the alienable one. But in Chamorro the suffix itself is overt. What distinguishes inalienable from alienable relations is not the presence or absence of a morpheme but the absence of an independent classifier in the inalienable construction.

Safford (1903: 511) describes an older pattern in which alienable possessive relations are mediated by a dependent noun (or 'possessive classifier' in Topping's 1973 terminology): iyo- for inanimate objects, and gâ- (probably from gåga 'animal') for animals, each inflected for person and number. Safford gives the paradigm iyoko / iyomo / iyoña 'my/your/his (possession)' for objects, and gâho / gâmo / gâña 'my/your/his (animal)' for pets and livestock. Chung (2020: 148–150) retains this three-way system (ga'-, na'- 'food', and iyu- 'possession') but reanalyses the elements as dependent nouns rather than classifiers, on the grounds that they can be incorporated into verbs of possession while full possessive NPs cannot. The system is less prominent in modern Chamorro than in Safford's description, possibly reflecting change under contact. Iyo- nonetheless survives in productive compounds such as iyon-tano 'terrestrial' (literally 'belonging of the earth').

The pattern is the expected one in terms of the Nichols (1988: 572) Inalienability Hierarchy: kinship terms and body parts are the most tightly bound to their possessor morphologically, while artefacts and abstract nouns are the least tightly bound and more readily take the linker or a dependent-noun construction.

This gradient pattern aligns with the typological generalisation that inalienable possession is typically head-marked while alienable possession is typically dependent-marked (Nichols 1992: 117): the inalienable end of the hierarchy uses suffix-only (head-marking) constructions, while the alienable end historically made use of the iyo- / gâ- system, which introduces an additional lexical element between possessor and possessee.

Two competing explanations for the cross-linguistic shape of the alienability split deserve mention. Haiman (1983) proposes an iconic account: less morphological material between possessor and possessee mirrors a closer conceptual bond. Haspelmath (2017) offers a frequency-based alternative: inalienable nouns like body parts and kinship terms occur with an overt possessor much more often than alienable nouns do, and this higher frequency favours morphological reduction through economy. The Chamorro data are compatible with both accounts and do not clearly distinguish between them, since the suffix-only construction is both formally shorter and associated with the highest-frequency possessive contexts. A corpus study of possessor-occurrence rates in Chamorro would be needed to test Haspelmath's prediction directly.

5.4Double possession and discourse effects

Chamorro allows what Topping (1973: 223) calls double possession — essentially a possessor-agreement suffix on the head and an overt full-NP possessor introduced by the personal article si. In (26), the third-person singular suffix on gima' 'house' is co-indexed with si Rosa.

This construction is typologically similar to what Karvovskaya (2018: 136–148) calls 'multiple-exponent possession', where both head-marking and dependent-flagging are used simultaneously. In Chamorro, the double-marked construction serves a discourse-pragmatic function: the overt possessor is introduced when it would not be recoverable from context alone, while the suffix on the head maintains the language's strong head-marking preference.

5.5Crosslinguistic context

Nichols (1992) and Nichols & Bickel (2013) place Chamorro among the head-marking Austronesian languages alongside Samoan, Fijian and Tongan. Chamorro's two-way strategy — possessor-agreement suffix plus linker — is typologically common in this family. What is more distinctive is the degree to which the older iyo- / gâ- / na'- dependent-noun system has been displaced or restructured in the modern language, and the fact that the linker -n is not a dedicated genitive but a general head-dependent marker. Together these features make the alienability split in Chamorro structural rather than categorical — consistent with Nichols's (1988) observation that the inalienability hierarchy is usually a gradient phenomenon in any given language.

06.
Summary

What the data reveal, what we could not test, and where the loose threads run deeper than this portfolio can reach.

Chamorro is an endangered Malayo-Polynesian language and, owing to prolonged colonial contact, one of the most heavily Hispanicised languages of the Pacific. Its word order is flexible VSO but rigid where morphosyntax alone cannot disambiguate arguments, and consistently head-initial across phrasal categories. The language permits a VOS alternative in contexts where ambiguity is low, suggesting that the variation is driven by interpretive clarity rather than free optionality. Chamorro's prepositional syntax conforms to Greenberg's (1963) Universal #3.

The alignment system is split by mood. Nominal case-marking is neutral; verbal person marking is ergative-absolutive in realis clauses and nominative-accusative in irrealis clauses; the weak-pronoun system tracks the verbal split. The productive antipassive in man-/fan- provides independent evidence for the ergative analysis. This mood-conditioned split is rare among Austronesian languages and only partially compatible with DeLancey's (1981) attention-flow model of split ergativity.

Comparative constructions follow the explicit strategy in Stassen's (2013) typology: a suffix -ña, a particle mås, and a standard marker kinu/ki encode the ordering directly, and the availability of differential measure phrases clinches the explicit analysis. Three of the four comparative exponents are Spanish borrowings — making Chamorro a useful test case for how contact can replace comparative morphology without altering the underlying typological strategy.

Adnominal possession is head-marking, with a possessor-agreement suffix for pronominal possessors and a linker -n for full NPs. The alienability split is gradient rather than categorical, following the Nichols (1988) Inalienability Hierarchy with kinship and body-part nouns at the tightly bound end, and the historically layered iyo- / gâ- classifier system at the alienable end.

6.1Limitations

The principal methodological limitation was working from secondary sources without access to a native-speaker consultant. Most examples are drawn from Topping (1973), Chung (1998, 2020) and Cooreman (1987, 1988), so empirical questions that require acceptability or frequency judgements could not be tested independently. WALS codings for Chamorro also rely disproportionately on Topping (1973) and may not reflect later corrections in Chung. General methodological guidance on glossing and elicitation follows Payne (1997).

6.2Directions for further research

Productive directions include: a corpus study of post-verbal argument ordering to quantify the VSO/VOS distribution; a closer investigation of the morphological status of -ña across comparative, possessive, and degree contexts; a study of the antipassive in spontaneous speech; and a description of the modern status of the alienability classifiers.

Each loose thread, like the trench beneath the islands themselves, runs deeper than a portfolio of this scope can reach.
